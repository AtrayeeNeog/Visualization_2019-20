1 Using visual analytics, compare the template subgraph with the potential matches provided. Show where the two graphs agree and disagree. Use your tool to answer the following questions:

  1. Compare the five candidate subgraphs to the provided template subgraph. Show where the two graphs agree and disagree. Which subgraph matches the template subgraph the best? Please limit your answer to seven images and 500 words.

Ans:

The fastest and easiest way to compare the subgraphs is using their network analysis and comparing their values for different measures. As you can see in Figure 1, some of these measures such as Degree, Betweenness and etc. are visualized as parallel coordinates (PCs) for all of the subgraphs. Each of the subgraphs were then compared with the template subgraph. Based on the certain patterns that emerges in these graphs, subgraphs 1 and 2 are seem to be the most similar to the template compared to the other subgraphs.

Figure 1. Parallel coordinates allow us to observe the distribution of all the similarity measures at the same time

 

In order to quantify these comparisons a Wasserstein-based test was done between each subgraph and the template subgraph for each measure. The Wasserstein metric is a distance measure that measures the similarity between two probability distributions by estimating the cost for moving or turning one distribution into another. The test outputs a Wasserstein metric and a p-Value which depends on three factors: size of the network, shape of the network and the location of the nodes in the network.

 

Comparisons in Figure 2 shows that subgraph 1 and 2 are overall more similar to the template subgraph due to more similar distributions, smaller Wasserstein metrics with higher confidence factor denoted by the p-value in most measures.

 

 

 

 

 

Figure 2. Comparison of subgraphs for each distribution measure. Distribution curves are compared in each cell. Wasserstein metric and p-Value are also written and besides contribution percentage of three factors (location, size and shape) are presented in form of pie charts.

 

 

 

 

Based on high p-values, three measures were selected to further investigate similarities and dissimilarities between the subgraphs. Figure 3, Figure 4 and Figure 5 visualize the network graphs of subgraphs. Color indicates the node Types: Person (blue), Product Category (red), Country (green), Financial Category and Document (yellow). Node sizes are proportional to the corresponding measures.

 

 

 

 

 

Figure 3. Out-Degree measures the number of edges going out for each node in a directed graph. In this particular case, out-going nodes usually belong to person IDs. Since the core of a hacker group is these persons, Out-Degrees help embolden the effect of people on the network analysis.

 

 

 

Figure 4. Betweenness Centrality measures the number of shortest paths passing through a node. It measures the importance of a node based on how centrally it is located in a network. Therefore, this measure helps us compare central and important nodes in the networks.

 

Figure 5. Eigen Centrality. In order to extract a network of hackers, who have created the outage, it is not only important to understand which nodes are central to the network but also how the central nodes are connected to each other. The Eigen Vector Centrality evaluates the nodes according to how many important nodes they are connected to. Nodes with high eigen vector score are directly connected.

Subgraph 1 and 2 are again the most similar to the template subgraph based on the above Network Graphs.

 

 

To enunciate these differences even more, an interactive plot is created which compares the Distributions of the template subgraph with the various Subgraphs for these three similarity measures.

 

 

 

Figure 6. distribution

 

For all the similarity measure distributions and the Wasserstein metrics values, subgraph 2 resembles the template subgraph the most except in the Eigen Vector Centrality. As shown in the pie-charts for the Matrix above, the main reason for the template subgraph and the Subgraph2 being different for the Eigen Vector Centrality is due to the difference in the shapes of the two Networks. The distances among the central or important nodes may have a wide range as compared to the distances between the central nodes for the template subgraph or Subgraph 1. This might be a cause for the difference in the shape of

 

 

 

 

these networks. However, if we look at the Page Rank similarity measure (which is the normalized Eigen Vector Centrality measure), this difference in the shape is compensated by the normalization and as a result has resulted in Subgraph 2 showing very high similarity with the template subgraph with very high confidence (p-values).

Figure 7. gephi

 

 

 

With all the above rationale in mind, we can conclude that Subgraph 2 is the most similar to the template subgraph.

The main objective of this task was to see if any of the Seeds given to us, leads to other potential networks that could have been involved. For this the first step was to extract all the nodes in every channel which were directly connected to these 3 Seeds. The networks obtained were very large and needed to be filtered further.

The second step was to divide the 3 networks into Channels and extract the persons who were central to these networks. In order to do this, initially the Degree Centrality measure was used to extract the nodes which had more than a minimum value for this measure in order to make the network a little smaller for analysis. The reduced networks were then analyzed on the basis of the patterns for the different channels that were retrieved from the question 1 analysis. Only those nodes which extensively followed the required patterns from each of these channels were kept in the final networks. It was seen that Seed 1 and Seed 3 led to two potential networks, but Seed 2 did not lead to any network and only was limited to the Co-Authorship Channel.

The graph building process for each of the seeds is depicted below as a four stage process:

        Level 1: The seeds are plotted

        Level 2: All nodes connected to the seed are plotted

        Extended: Selected the nodes (persons) relevant for the Networks (through a minimum threshold criteria for Closeness Centrality); all nodes connected to these clusters were added

        Reduced: Clusters were retrieved from the Channel Analysis to select only noes following channel patterns

5 What was the greatest challenge you had when working with the large graph data? How did you overcome that difficulty? What could make it easier to work with this kind of data?

The difficulties faced with the large graph data were as follows:

        Loading the entire dataset: Not many libraries are recognized which are able to load this huge amount of data efficiently.

        Memory: The dataset was too large to store in the RAM and needed to be saved in the hard-drive which costed computational time.

        Computational Complexity: The dataset was too large and complex and required a large amount of computational time.

        Complexity in Interpretation: The dataset was too large and complex to visualize or interpret in one go and needed to be divided into parts for better interpretability. Also, defining the whole dataset as a graph dataframe and finding subgraphs or any other graph related characteristics was computationally impossible.

Solutions:

        R libraries like fread and ff package were able to load the large dataset quickly.

        Using online tools like Colab helped optimize the use of RAM and gave us more computational power.

        Dividing the dataset into Channels and working on the patterns, helped segment the data for a better interpretation.

What could make it easier to work with this kind of data?

        Larger RAMs

        Libraries or Tools which can do the computations in the GPU
